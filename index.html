I am a biologist broadly interested in the dynamics of microbial populations and complex gene regulatory neteworks. I also love coding and electronics as hobbies… so it didn’t take long before I started to use them for my work. While my interest in microbes revolve around how rapid they can become resistant to antimicrobials, or the regulatory network of efflux pumps, I also love to develop (and 3D print!) new gadgets that can be used in the laboratory or commercially. In the past I co-developed a compact spectrophotometer (more below!), and currently I work Prof Lars Steinmetz’s laboratory, in the Department of Genetics at Stanford University, where I use impedance spectroscopy to detect SARS-CoV-2.

If you want to know how a biologist ended up involved in technology development and software engineering, or if you just want to say hi, drop me an email to reding [at] stanford.edu or get in touch through twitter.

Projects

  • What antibiotic concentrations lead to the fastest evolution of resistance? Resistant mutants have annoyingly followed the introduction of virtually every antibiotic introduced into the clinic since the discovery of penicillin. Why? The world of antibiotic use can be incredibly convoluted due to the lack of formal rationales to define the duration or dose of antibiotic therapies. Whence the variation (and contradictions!) guidelines of different countries across the globe about how to use these drugs. In this project I combined mathematical modelling, experimental evolution and genome sequencing to determine—and more importantly redict—which antibiotic concentrations will lead to resistant fastest. Conventional wisdom holds that resistant mutants emerge between two arbitrary drug concentrations that define the mutant selection window. What we found is that, for a given species, there is a continuum of mutation windows many of which fall outside of the traditional mutant selection window. For details… read the paper (link on the title)! A cool detail about the paper is that we foud de novo mutants within 3 days after exposure to erythromycin with amplifications of the operon acr (responsible for the multi-drug efflux pump AcrAB-TolC). Mutation and timing eerily similar to clinical data.
  • Evolution of antibiotic resistance in 2D space Antibiotic adaptation is commonly studied with experimental setups that keep drug concentration constant, say, serial dilutions in test tubes or microtitre plates. Inside the body, or in the environment, antibiotics diffuse and their concentration changes over time. How does this circumstance shape the evolution of E. coli and how does it compare to conventional experimental setups? In this project we combined mathematical modelling, image analysis, software engineering and a computerised camera to track the growth and evolution of Escherichia coli growing on agar plates, in assays that resemble antibiograms. We found that antibiotics create reservoirs of carbon due to the inhibition they exert on the becterium. Combined with the progressive exposure to the drug in cells growing beyond the immediate viccinity of the antibiotic, due diffusion, we found the first signs of resistance within 24h. When we used a construct labelled with green fluorescent protein, we could track the relative abundance of mutants with increasing copies of the operon acr and verify that Ronald Fisher was kind of right in the 1930s with his thing about benefitial mutations expanding as waves! More details in the paper / preprint!
  • Biodynamic Devices & the Light Modulator In this project I explored along Profs Ivana Gudelj and Robert Beardmore the use of image analysis to develop an affordable spectrophotometer, which led to the creation of Biodynamic Devices. It’s patent-pending (see below!), so all I can say is that here I developed my python skills with the development of the device’s backend, API, communication stack, and hardware control along with electrical engineering skills. A very fun project indeed!
